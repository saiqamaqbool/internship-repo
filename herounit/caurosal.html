1. 1903 Kashmir floods

Regarded at the time as the "greatest flood ever known," this event was a major turning point in the region's approach to flood control. Cause: Incessant rainfall in July 1903 caused the Jhelum River to rise to a maximum level of 5,200 feet. Impact: Srinagar was submerged, with the Dal Lake's protective embankment breached and floating gardens destroyed. Approximately 7,000 homes in and around Srinagar collapsed, and over 40 miles of roads were submerged. The disaster prompted the first proper, scientifically-based flood control measures in the valley, including the excavation of a spill channel and the start of dredging operations.

2. 1992 India–Pakistan floods

Caused by heavy monsoon rains in September, this flood affected regions across northern India and Pakistan, including Jammu and Kashmir. Cause: Severe monsoon rains caused the Indus, Chenab, and Jhelum rivers to overflow. Impact: In Jammu and Kashmir, 200 people were killed and more than 2,600 villages were affected, with nearly 400 completely submerged. Water levels in some urban areas reached up to 20 feet. Bad weather significantly hampered initial rescue operation.

3. 2014 Jammu and Kashmir floods

The most recent major flood was a catastrophic disaster triggered by torrential rainfall, overwhelming the region's infrastructure. Cause: Heavy and incessant monsoon rainfall in early September led to the overflowing of the Jhelum River and other streams. Impact: At least 277 people died in India, with Srinagar experiencing extensive damage from unplanned urban development and river encroachment. Vital infrastructure, including roads and over 30 bridges, was washed away, disrupting relief efforts. More than 2,600 villages were affected, and entire houses and hotels in Srinagar's Rajbagh area were submerged. The total estimated economic loss for the Kashmir division was over ₹1 trillion
